## 1. Study Design
**Recommendation:** Three-stage design. (1) Retrospective external validation on multi-site data with a **temporal holdout** (train on earlier encounters, test on later ones) to simulate deployment-time drift. (2) Prospective **silent-mode (shadow) deployment** in target outpatient and collaborative-care clinics, scoring live encounters without surfacing outputs, comparing predicted trajectory class against subsequently observed symptom-scale trajectories. (3) If the team wants to claim the model *improves treatment intensification decisions* (not merely predicts trajectory), a pragmatic **cluster-randomized or stepped-wedge trial** at the clinic level, with outcomes such as time-to-intensification, symptom remission/response rates, and clinician override rates.
**Rationale:** The retrieved literature shows the current state of clinical NLP validation is dominated by internal/development-stage validation — e.g., the AquaAI transformer model reports only internal validation on prehospital records (PMID 42265769), and the systematic review of NLP/LLM phenotyping notes limited external validation as a field-wide gap (PMID 41990239). Documentation style is site- and language-specific, so single-site retrospective performance does not generalize; multi-site external and temporal validation is the minimum credible evidence for a prediction claim. A guidance claim (changing treatment decisions) requires an interventional comparison; no matching interventional trial for MDD trajectory-stratification NLP was retrieved from ClinicalTrials.gov in this pull.

## 2. Sensor / Input Validation (Pre-Condition)
**Recommendation:** No physical sensor is consumed; the input-validity threat is **documentation and data-pipeline integrity**, and it must be validated before any downstream claim. Required pre-condition studies: (a) EHR extraction fidelity — verify that note text, questionnaire responses, and structured features arrive at the model uncorrupted and time-aligned (correct encounter, correct patient); (b) characterization of template text, copy-forward/cloned documentation, smart-phrase boilerplate, and scribe/dictation artifacts, with quantified model sensitivity to each; (c) questionnaire-response completeness and modality (portal-entered vs. clinician-transcribed vs. verbally administered) as a pre-specified input covariate; (d) missing-data behavior of the tabular branch (absent prior scale scores, incomplete medication history) with defined fallback behavior — the model must not silently emit a confident trajectory class when its structured inputs are largely missing.
**Rationale:** Clinical-text NLP performance is known to be tightly coupled to documentation conventions; the clinical-text-embedding systematic review flags representation sensitivity to source text characteristics (PMID 42214285), and transformer models over EHR notes show pipeline-dependent performance in ADE detection (PMID 42384713). Copy-forward text can inject stale symptom descriptions that masquerade as current severity — a direct, mechanistic threat to trajectory prediction.

## 4. Ground Truth Strategy
**Recommendation:** Define trajectory classes **prospectively and pre-registered**, derived from serial administrations of a validated symptom scale (e.g., PHQ-9 or MADRS series over a fixed follow-up window), with explicit rules for minimum number of observations, window length, and handling of irregular sampling. Do not use clinician impression notes as ground truth — they are the model's *input* and would create label leakage. Adjudicate ambiguous trajectories (e.g., sparse follow-up, treatment switching) via a blinded clinician panel with documented inter-rater agreement.
**Rationale:** Label reliability is a ceiling on achievable performance. PHQ-9-type self-report scales have known test–retest noise and administration-mode effects; patients lost to follow-up create informative missingness (dropouts may be the deteriorating patients the model most needs to catch). Trajectory labels built from sparse or irregular scale data are inherently noisy, and reported sensitivity/specificity cannot exceed what that noise permits — this must be stated in the protocol and quantified (e.g., via label-noise sensitivity analysis). No retrieved record establishes a reference-standard definition of "severity trajectory class" for MDD; the definition itself is a study artifact that must be defended.

## 6. Subgroup Requirements
**Recommendation:** Pre-specify and separately power, at minimum: (a) **race/ethnicity and primary language** — clinical language models encode demographic and dialectal documentation differences, and questionnaire text from non-native English speakers or translated instruments shifts the input distribution (multilingual transfer is non-trivial; cf. mT5, DOI 10.18653/v1/2021.naacl-main.41); (b) **sex/gender and age** (older adults present depression with more somatic/sleep phenotypes — directly interacting with the tabular sleep/psychomotor features); (c) **site and documentation style** — specialty behavioral-health notes vs. primary-care notes differ systematically in length, structure, and symptom vocabulary; treat site as a stratification variable, not a nuisance; (d) **questionnaire administration mode** (portal self-entry vs. clinician-administered); (e) **comorbidity strata** (anxiety disorders, substance use, bipolar spectrum — where trajectory semantics differ and misclassification is higher-stakes). Critically: the **adversarial debiasing must be empirically verified on held-out deployment-distribution data** — report per-subgroup error rates and calibration, not just training-time fairness losses; debiasing in training does not guarantee error parity under distribution shift.
**Rationale:** The population-specific validity threat here is linguistic and documentation physics: the model reads *how patients and clinicians write*, so any systematic difference in expression, literacy, language, or note style is a mechanism for differential error. The debiasing component is a design intent, not evidence; the validation study is where the parity claim is either earned or falsified.

## 8. Post-Deployment Monitoring
**Recommendation:** Deploy with a monitoring plan covering: (a) **input drift** — statistical monitoring of note length, vocabulary distribution, template prevalence, questionnaire completion rates, and structured-feature missingness against validation-cohort baselines (EHR upgrades, new scribes, or new templates are known drift triggers); (b) **outcome-linked calibration surveillance** — rolling comparison of predicted trajectory class vs. subsequently observed symptom-scale trajectories, reported per subgroup to detect emerging error disparities post-debiasing; (c) **clinician-override and alert-response logging** — override rates and reasons as an early signal of miscalibration or automation-bias in either direction; (d) **safety-event pathway** — a defined process for reporting harms (e.g., delayed intensification in a deteriorating patient predicted stable), analogous in spirit to MAUDE reporting for cleared devices; retrieved MAUDE/recall records (e.g., recall Z-1672-2021; MAUDE report 1062083) are hardware-related and not relevant precedents but illustrate the malfunction-reporting discipline expected; (e) **pre-specified retraining, rollback, and re-validation triggers**, with any retrained model treated as a change requiring at minimum silent-mode re-verification (and, if FDA-cleared, handled under a Predetermined Change Control Plan).
**Rationale:** NLP systems degrade with documentation practice changes faster than most sensor-based models; the hallucination/robustness literature underscores that language-model failure modes are input-distribution-dependent (DOI 10.1145/3571730). Because the model influences treatment intensification, feedback loops are possible (model-driven intensification changes the very trajectories used as future labels) — the monitoring plan must account for outcome contamination.

## What This Validation Certifies — and What It Does Not
**CERTIFIES:** That, in adult outpatients with MDD in the studied behavioral-health and collaborative-care sites, the model's predicted symptom-severity trajectory class agrees with subsequently observed, scale-derived trajectories at the pre-specified performance and calibration targets, including within the pre-specified demographic, language, site, and comorbidity subgroups — and (if the interventional stage is run) that surfacing those predictions changed intensification decisions as measured.
**DOES NOT CERTIFY:**

- That the trajectory-class definitions themselves are the clinically optimal way to segment depression course — they are a study construct bounded by symptom-scale label noise.
- Generalization to inpatient, pediatric, non-English, or documentation environments (EHR systems, templates, languages) not represented in the validation cohorts.
- That adversarial debiasing guarantees error parity beyond the tested subgroups and time period.
